Working at Wowza was my first opportunity to contribute to strategic planning in a full time design role, reporting directly to the VP of Product and meeting on a regular cadence with the CEO.

Filling the Funnel

Joining as the sole designer at a 150+ person company meant getting up to speed quickly. Early weeks were spent gathering landscape intel — reviewing analytics, sitting in on sales calls, and conducting informal interviews with customer success and marketing. The goal was to understand where the product was losing people before building anything new.

Those conversations surfaced three emerging priorities that would shape the work ahead: increase sales funnel conversions through an improved trial experience, continue refining the UI redesign and product rebrand already underway, and improve an inefficient internal process for capturing and communicating product requirements across teams.

Discovery phase — landscape research and priority mapping

Choose Your Own Adventure

Trial abandonment was the most pressing conversion problem. Outdated visuals and complicated form fields were creating friction before users ever reached the product. A competitive analysis of peer platforms revealed how far the trial onboarding experience had fallen behind — most competitors had reduced their sign-up to under three fields and led immediately into a guided first-use moment.

To move quickly and build internal alignment, I facilitated a two-day onsite working session with stakeholders from product, marketing, and sales. The goal was to map the full trial journey, identify drop-off moments, and generate ideas across disciplines rather than passing documents back and forth. What came out of it shaped the redesigned trial flow directly.

Interfacing

The visual redesign of Wowza Video was already in motion when I joined — brand direction had been established, but the execution was fragmented. My first task was importing the existing Sketch files into Figma and establishing a working component library that engineering could reference consistently. That alone reduced the back-and-forth on implementation questions significantly.

One of the more impactful early wins was solving the empty dashboard problem. New users landing in an empty product with no guidance were churning before reaching their first value moment. I designed a system of contextual prompts and getting-started links that surfaced the right action at the right time — reducing the gap between sign-up and first meaningful use.

Before — fragmented visual language After — unified Figma component system
  • Improved navigation contrast and hierarchy to reduce cognitive load across the primary sidebar
  • Removed the persistent trial banner that was visually dominating the interface and creating anxiety rather than urgency
  • Established a clearer visual hierarchy within the content panel so users could orient quickly to the task at hand
  • Introduced useful getting started content into the empty state experience to guide new users toward their first action

Cross-functional Communication

One of the more unexpected dimensions of this role was stepping into a cross-functional facilitator position. As the sole designer embedded in the product organization, I became a natural connective point between departments that weren't communicating efficiently — particularly product, engineering, and marketing.

I introduced a lightweight requirements process that gave each team a consistent format for submitting and reviewing work, replacing informal Slack threads and misaligned email chains. Whiteboarding sessions that had previously been ad-hoc became structured touchpoints with clear outputs. Over time, the process improvements were as visible as the design work itself.

Closing Thoughts

This role reinforced something I carry into every engagement: clearly defined goals and genuine team alignment are prerequisites for good design work, not outcomes of it. Being included in strategic conversations from the start — sitting in the room with the VP of Product and the CEO — meant the work was grounded in business reality rather than design assumptions. Looking back, the value created here wasn't just in screens shipped, but in the connective tissue built between teams that hadn't previously had a shared language.
