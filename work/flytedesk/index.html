Role

Digital Product Design

Timeline

Context

Full-time internship · Agile team

Spending the summer working with Flytedesk was a valuable and eye-opening introduction to the daily life of a designer out in the wild.

The internship meant wearing multiple hats in a fast-moving startup environment. A single engineering team led by the VP of Product and one product designer defined the rhythm of the work — tasks were scoped in direct support of their efforts, touching multiple projects over the course of the summer rather than going deep on a single feature. That breadth turned out to be one of the most valuable parts of the experience.

Flytedesk 101

Flytedesk connects local businesses and national brands with college media advertising opportunities — giving advertisers a single platform to reach the college audience across a wide range of formats. Alongside traditional print and digital ads, the platform offered on-campus placements including billboards, digital signs, stickers, and street teams, all purchasable through the platform's Ad Shop. The breadth of inventory was impressive and, it turned out, part of what made the product so complex to design for.

Flytedesk platform — Ad Shop and inventory overview

Inventory and Tagging: Admin Tracking Tables

One of the first projects was designing low fidelity wireframes for internal admins to track and manage tags — representations of advertising inventory across the platform. The work reinforced an appreciation for well-organized data and surfaced the real difficulty of constraining large amounts of content into a single coherent view without losing context or creating new friction.

Low fidelity wireframes — admin tag tracking table iterations

User testing with internal stakeholders uncovered three specific needs that shaped the next iteration: a simple way to assign user-generated tags to a global super tag for organizational clarity, the ability to exclude irrelevant tags from a given view, and an option to create and manage new products directly within the table rather than navigating away.

Before — initial table layout After — refined with user testing findings

Continued work on the inventory page led to the introduction of a slide-out drawer with tabs for critical user actions — a pattern that kept the table uncluttered while surfacing the full range of controls when needed. The solution was later approved and released to production.

Slide-out drawer — tabbed actions panel, approved for production

User Dashboard

Attending cross-departmental meetings early on provided a broader view of the company's needs beyond any single feature. During a brainstorming session focused on customer communication, it became clear that a high volume of transactional emails was creating noise for users and support overhead for the team. Suggesting a dashboard as a way to consolidate that information landed well — the idea evolved through power-user interviews and ultimately into a high fidelity mockup. A basic MVP of the dashboard concept went live in production in November 2019.

Dashboard — high fidelity mockup, MVP released Nov 2019

The dashboard was designed to enable users to access pending actions, verifications, and general notifications alongside trackable campaign statuses — all from a single view. Rather than hunting through emails or navigating multiple sections of the platform, users could arrive at a meaningful overview of everything that needed their attention.

Dashboard — component explorations and layout iterations

A second round of user testing following the low fidelity wireframes revealed a clear preference: users wanted notifications as the main focal point rather than campaign status. That insight reordered the hierarchy of the dashboard and informed which elements received the most visual weight. The long-term vision was a fully customizable dashboard with user-configurable widgets, giving different personas — advertisers, publishers, admins — the ability to tailor their view to their own workflow.

Dashboard — notification-first layout after second round of user testing

Responsive Ad Shop

The Ad Shop had been built and optimized for desktop — but local business advertising sales often happened in the field, on tablets and phones. That disconnect was a real problem. Starting with sketches of the existing desktop Ad Shop to understand its structure, I then moved into brainstorming sessions to explore how the layout could adapt to small screen constraints without losing the browsability that made the shop functional.

Desktop Ad Shop — sketches and structural analysis Small screen — layout brainstorm explorations

Digital mockups followed for both mobile and tablet screen sizes, working through the specific layout decisions each breakpoint required.

Responsive Ad Shop — mobile and tablet mockups

The exploration also surfaced options for new mobile-friendly display formats, including publisher branding treatments that could give the Ad Shop a more tailored feel for different college media networks.

Mobile display options — publisher branding explorations
Publisher branding — alternative display treatments

The responsive Ad Shop ultimately fell outside the scope of what could be completed within the internship timeline. Identified next steps included contextual inquiry with publisher field reps to better understand how they used the platform in real selling situations, and dedicated research on the local buyer persona to inform layout priorities for a future responsive release.

Summary

The Flytedesk team was consistently looking for ways to improve and genuinely open to suggestions — even from an intern. That openness made the work feel meaningful rather than peripheral. The complexity of the college media world also made the product more nuanced than anticipated: the range of inventory types, buyer personas, and publisher relationships meant that almost every design decision had a wrinkle worth examining. Most memorably, the value of constant dialogue with satisfied customers became concrete here — a half hour chat with a power user provided more actionable insight than hours of internal speculation.
